Toribrot Module
1. Base Surface: Complex Torus / Elliptic Curve
Fix a lattice (\Lambda = \langle \omega_1,\omega_2\rangle \subset \mathbb{C}) with (\Im(\omega_2/\omega_1) > 0).
Define the complex torus: [ T^2(\tau) = \mathbb{C} / \Lambda,\quad \tau = \omega_2/\omega_1. ]
By the uniformization theorem for elliptic curves, every complex torus (\mathbb{C}/\Lambda) admits the structure of a complex elliptic curve, unique up to isomorphism. Thus there exists a complex‑analytic isomorphism [ \phi: T^2(\tau) \xrightarrow{;\cong;} E(\mathbb{C}), ] where (E) is an elliptic curve in Weierstrass form. We identify (T^2(\tau)) with (E(\mathbb{C})) via (\phi) and equip it with the elliptic curve group law.

2. Intrinsic Torus Maps
Toribrot‑Geometric uses two families of intrinsic maps.

2.1. Elliptic Endomorphism with Translation (Affine Self‑Map)
Let (E(\mathbb{C})) be the elliptic curve corresponding to (T^2(\tau)).

For an integer (m), let [ [m]: E(\mathbb{C}) \to E(\mathbb{C}) ] be the elliptic curve endomorphism “multiplication by (m)” in the group law.

This is a finite holomorphic map of degree [ \deg([m]) = m^2. ]

Fix (|m|\ge 2).

For a point (P \in E(\mathbb{C})), define [ f_{P,m}: E(\mathbb{C}) \to E(\mathbb{C}),\quad f_{P,m}(Q) = [m]Q + P. ]

Each (f_{P,m}) is a holomorphic affine self‑map of the elliptic curve: it is the composition of the group endomorphism ([m]) with a translation by (P). It is a group endomorphism if and only if (P = 0); for (P\neq 0), it is not a group homomorphism but remains a finite holomorphic map of degree (m^2). Iterating (f_{P,m}) defines one class of Toribrot‑Geometric dynamics.

2.2. Hyperbolic Toral Automorphisms (SL(2,ℤ) Mode, Smooth Category)
To define the SL(2,ℤ) mode, use a real model of the torus:

Choose (v_1, v_2 \in \mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2) such that [ \Lambda = \mathbb{Z} v_1 \oplus \mathbb{Z} v_2, ] and identify (T^2(\tau)) with (\mathbb{R}^2/\mathbb{Z}^2) using this basis.

Let (A \in SL(2,\mathbb{Z})) be a real (2\times 2) matrix with determinant 1.

Assume (A) is hyperbolic: its eigenvalues (\lambda,\lambda^{-1}) satisfy (|\lambda|>1); let (\lambda) denote the expanding eigenvalue.

Define [ F_A: T^2(\tau) \to T^2(\tau) ] as the map induced by (x \mapsto A x) on (\mathbb{R}^2) modulo (\mathbb{Z}^2) in the chosen basis.

The induced map (F_A) depends on the chosen identification of (T^2(\tau)) with (\mathbb{R}^2/\mathbb{Z}^2), but different choices correspond via conjugation by torus diffeomorphisms.

In this SL(2,ℤ) mode, we work in the smooth (C^\infty) category on the real torus. The map (F_A) is generally not holomorphic with respect to the complex structure on (T^2(\tau)), unless (A) happens to respect that complex structure (which is exceptional).

Iterating (F_A) defines another class of Toribrot‑Geometric dynamics.

3. Pants Decomposition and Composition
3.1. Topological Pants on the Torus
A pair of pants is a compact surface homeomorphic to a sphere with three disjoint open disks removed; equivalently, a genus‑0 surface with three boundary components.

We consider:

Open subsets (U \subset T^2(\tau)),
Embeddings [ i: P \hookrightarrow U \subset T^2(\tau), ] where (P) is a pair of pants and the boundary components of (P) map to simple closed curves in (T^2(\tau)).
These pants domains are local domains for operations.

3.2. Pants Units (Local Operations)
A pants unit consists of:

A pair‑of‑pants domain (P \subset T^2(\tau)),
A map (\Phi: P \to T^2(\tau)), typically the restriction of one of the intrinsic maps (holomorphic (f_{P,m}) or smooth (F_A)) to (P) or a subdomain thereof.
The three boundary components of (P) act as interfaces. Depending on the direction of composition, two boundaries may be treated as inputs and one as output, or vice versa.

3.3. Functional Composition on Open Sets
Global Toribrot‑Geometric structures are formed by gluing pants units along boundary components and composing their maps.

Let ({(P_i, \Phi_i)}) be pants units with (P_i \subset T^2(\tau)).
Suppose boundary components of (P_i) and (P_j) are identified (glued) along a common simple closed curve (C \subset T^2(\tau)), forming a larger domain (U \subset T^2(\tau)).
Compatibility condition:
On each glued boundary component (C), the corresponding maps must satisfy:

Either (\Phi_i|_C = \Phi_j|_C), or
(\Phi_i|_C) and (\Phi_j|_C) differ by a diffeomorphism of (C) that extends to a diffeomorphism of a collar neighborhood of (C) in (T^2(\tau)).
Under this condition, one can define combined maps on unions of pants domains and extend by functional composition on overlapping open sets.

3.4. Pants Composition Graph
Each pants unit ((P_i,\Phi_i)) is represented as a node in a directed graph.
Directed edges represent the flow from input boundaries to output boundaries under gluing and composition.
The pants composition graph is required to be:

Directed, and
Acyclic, unless one explicitly constructs periodic dynamical structures—in which case directed cycles may be deliberately introduced to represent periodicity.
This graph encodes a decomposition with potentially unbounded depth and local branching constrained by the three‑boundary structure of pants.

4. Metric and Texture Fields
Let (g_0) be an initial Riemannian metric on (T^2(\tau)).

Under a hyperbolic toral automorphism (F_A) with expanding eigenvalue (\lambda), define a sequence of metrics by pullback‑renormalization: [ g_{n+1} = \lambda^{-2} (F_A)^* g_n. ]

Interpretation:

Since (\det A = 1), the toral automorphism (F_A) preserves total area on (T^2(\tau)).
The pullback ((F_A)^* g_n) expands lengths by (\lambda) along the unstable direction and contracts by (\lambda^{-1}) along the stable direction (in the linear model).
The renormalization factor (\lambda^{-2}) is introduced to normalize the growth along the unstable direction under iteration, helping to keep the metric bounded in that direction; it is not required for area preservation (which holds already because (\det A = 1)).
Derived scalar fields (e.g., norms, distortion measures, curvature proxies) obtained from ({g_n}) can be defined on open sets and restricted to pants domains. These fields are carried along and updated under the dynamics.

Enumerated Toribrot Specification (Root = 0)
1. Addresses and Generations
Each node has a finite binary address: [ a = (b_1, b_2, \dots, b_k),\quad b_i \in {0,1},\ k \ge 1. ]
The root node has address: [ a_{\text{root}} = [0]. ]
The generation (depth) of a node with address (a) is: [ \mathrm{gen}(a) = \text{len}(a) - 1. ] So:
(\mathrm{gen}([0]) = 0) for the root,
children of root have addresses [0,0] and [0,1] and generation 1, etc.
2. Tree Structure
Nodes are indexed by finite binary lists starting with 0:
Root: [0].
For any node with address a, its children have addresses:
a + [0],
a + [1].
Parent/child relation:
The parent of a node with address a (length ≥ 2) is the node with address a[:-1] (drop the last bit).
The root [0] has no parent.
This gives a rooted full binary tree with:

Root at [0],
Unique path from root to any node,
generation = len(address) - 1.
3. Node Data
Each node stores:

address: list[int] of 0/1, with root [0],
generation: int = len(address) - 1,
children: list[Node], either empty or [left, right],
value: optional arithmetic value (default None),
fields: dict[str, Any] for scalar fields (prime flag, log, gap, Λ, etc.).

Here is the revised class and helper functions:

class Node:
    def __init__(self, address_bits):
        # address_bits: list of 0/1, root is [0]
        self.address = address_bits
        # generation: root [0] -> 0, children [0,0],[0,1] -> 1, etc.
        self.generation = len(address_bits) - 1
        self.children = []      # will be [left, right] or []
        self.value = None       # optional arithmetic value
        self.fields = {}        # arbitrary scalar fields

def create_root():
    # root node has address [0]
    return Node([0])

def expand(node):
    left  = Node(node.address + [0])
    right = Node(node.address + [1])
    node.children = [left, right]
    return left, right
This:

Uses [0] as the root address,
Treats the first element 0 as the root label,
Keeps the binary expansion rule consistent.

<Zetabrot>
Zetabrot — Definition (Built on Enumerated Toribrot)
I. Base: Enumerated Toribrot
Start from the Enumerated Toribrot structure:

Nodes (Brots)

For each integer (n \ge 2), there is exactly one brot node.
The node is indexed by the integer (n).
No duplication: one brot = one integer.
Composite Pants (Factor Structure)

For a composite (n), choose a factorization (n = ab) with (1 < a \le b).
Attach a pants unit:
Waist: node (n),
Legs: nodes (a) and (b).
If multiple factorizations are used, pants stack in series, not parallel: [ n \to a_1 b_1 \to a_2 b_2 \to \cdots ]
This recursively decomposes (n) down to primes, forming a loop‑back circuit to prime seeds.
Prime Nodes

For a prime (p), there are no factor pants; instead, the node receives parameter pants (arithmetic observables).
Prime Parameters
For the (n)-th prime (p_n), attach:

Log parameter: (L_n = \log p_n),
Gap parameter: (g_n = p_{n+1} - p_n),
Optional canonical field: the von Mangoldt function (\Lambda(n)), extended to all integers: [ \Lambda(n) = \begin{cases} \log p & \text{if } n = p^k \text{ for some prime } p, k\ge 1,\[2pt] 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} ]
Field Layer

Define a scalar field (a(n)) over all integer brots:
e.g. prime indicator, gap‑derived function, or (\Lambda(n)).
This makes the Toribrot an integer‑indexed scalar field with a pants‑style factorization structure.
II. Zetabrot: Operator & Spectral Construction
The Zetabrot is the operator‑spectral layer built from the Enumerated Toribrot field.

1. Dirichlet/Mellin Operator (Brot → Spectral Map)
Given a field (a(n)) on brots, define the Dirichlet/Mellin transform:

[ F(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a(n),n^{-s},\quad s = \sigma + it. ]

Interpretation in pants language:

One leg: field value (a(n)),
Other leg: kernel (n^{-s}),
Waist: summation over all brot nodes (n).
This operator (F(s)) is the Zetabrot transform of the Toribrot field.

Special case (Λ‑Zetabrot):

If (a(n) = \Lambda(n)), then for (\Re(s) > 1),

[ F(s) = \sum_{n\ge 1} \Lambda(n),n^{-s} = -\frac{\zeta'(s)}{\zeta(s)}. ]

This is the Λ‑Zetabrot, directly linked to the zeta zeros.

2. Log-Domain Compression (Zetabrot Signal)
Introduce log‑time variable:

[ u = \log n. ]

Define the Zetabrot spike train:

[ G(u) = \sum_{n\ge 1} a(n),\delta(u - \log n). ]

This is the Toribrot field compressed onto the log axis:

Each brot (n) becomes a spike at (u = \log n) with weight (a(n)).
3. Fourier Layer (Zeta Ear / Zetabrot Phasor Sum)
Define the Fourier transform of (G):

[ \widehat{G}(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} G(u),e^{-itu},du = \sum_{n\ge 1} a(n),e^{-it\log n}. ]

With damping exponent (\sigma), this becomes:

[ \widehat{G}(t;\sigma) = \sum_{n\ge 1} a(n),n^{-\sigma} e^{-it\log n} = \sum_{n\ge 1} a(n),n^{-\sigma - it}. ]

Λ‑Zetabrot Fourier form:

For (a(n)=\Lambda(n)):

[ \widehat{G}(t;\sigma) = \sum_{n\ge 1} \Lambda(n),n^{-\sigma - it} = -\frac{\zeta'(\sigma+it)}{\zeta(\sigma+it)}. ]

Each term:

[ \Lambda(n),n^{-\sigma} e^{-it\log n} ]

is a rotating phasor with angular velocity (\log n) and amplitude controlled by (n^{-\sigma}) and (\Lambda(n)).

This is the Zetabrot Ear / Oscilloscope: a spectral readout of the Toribrot integer field in log‑time.

4. Spectral Layer (Zeros & Explicit Formula)
In the Λ‑Zetabrot case, the transform (-\zeta'(s)/\zeta(s)) has poles at the non‑trivial zeros (\rho = \beta + i\gamma) of (\zeta(s)). The explicit formula connects:

Arithmetic domain (Toribrot field over integers / primes),
Spectral domain (zeros (\rho)),
with each zero contributing a term of the form:

[ x^\rho = x^\beta e^{i\gamma\log x} ]

to prime‑counting fluctuations.

The Riemann Hypothesis becomes a Zetabrot spectral amplitude constraint:

[ \beta = \tfrac12 \quad \forall \rho, ]

i.e., all non‑trivial zeros lie on the critical line (\Re(s) = \tfrac12), meaning every spectral contribution has magnitude (x^{1/2}).

III. Summary: What Zetabrot Is
Input: The Enumerated Toribrot (one node per integer), with:

pants‑style factor structure for composites,
parameter pants for primes,
a chosen scalar field (a(n)) (especially (a(n)=\Lambda(n))).
Zetabrot Construction:

Form the Dirichlet/Mellin transform (F(s)=\sum a(n)n^{-s}).
Interpret this via log‑domain spike train (G(u)).
Take the Fourier transform (\widehat{G}(t;\sigma)) = Zetabrot Ear / Oscilloscope.
In the Λ‑case, this is (-\zeta'/\zeta), whose singularities encode the zeta zeros.
Zetabrot is thus:

The operator‑spectral transform of the Enumerated Toribrot integer field, realized as a log‑domain Fourier/Mellin phasor sum whose poles and oscillations are governed by the zeros of the Riemann zeta function.
